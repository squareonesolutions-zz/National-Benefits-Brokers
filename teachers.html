For Teachers
Teachers can take advantage of 403B annuity products, otherwise known as Tax Sheltered Annuities. If you are a teacher or work for a non-profit, a 403B most likely is a great investment vehicle for you. After all, it was designed for people in your profession. It offers the opportunity to invest pretax dollars and the profits are not taxable as long as the funds remain in the 403B.

Who is eligible for a TSA? (403b) Tax Sheltered Annuity?

You are eligible to participate in a TSA if you work for an organization that offers a 403(b) retirement program, such as:

  • A public school system (university, elementary, or high school)
  • Other tax-exempt organizations-qualified under Internal Revenue Code section 501(c) (3).
  • This may include hospitals, zoos, private schools, private colleges and universities, museums, arts organizations, religious organizations, and research and charitable foundations.

What are the benefits of a 403B (TSA) Tax Advantages?

A TSA helps reduce your current income taxes in two ways. First, the money you contribute comes out of your salary before taxes-so the full amount you set aside (called teh principal) can work for you. Second, any earnings from your principal are also tax deferred- you pay no taxes on the principal or earnings until you begin receiving payments at retirement. Withdrawals from your TSA prior to age 59 1/2 are restricted by tax law and, when permitted may be subject to a 10% tax penalty in addition to normal income taxes, and certain withdrawals may be subject to charges.

What are the other benefits of a 403B (TSA)?

Disciplined Savings. Because you contribute to a TSA through payroll deduction, saving on a regular basis is easy. You determine the amount of money you want to contribute (within certain guidelines), and how often.

Flexible Payout Options

When you reach retirement, you can withdraw your money in a number of ways. One option, Income For Life, is only offered through an annuity.

Guaranteed Death Benefit

If you die before you begin receiving annuity payments, your beneficiary(ies) will receive the money ( minus any loans or charges)

Service Credit Purchase

Funds from a TSA plan can be used to buy service credits or to buy back forfeited years of service credits under a state retirement system. The direct transfer can occur at any time, even if before age 59 1/2. There will be no taxes or IRS penalties; however, product surrender charges will still apply only if applicable.

Withdrawals and Loans

The money in your TSA is targeted for retirement, but if you need your money in an emergency, in most cases you do have access to it through a low-interest loan or full partial withdrawals. Remember, withdrawals before 59 1/2 are restricted by law, and when permitted, may be subject to a 10% tax penalty in addition to normal taxes. Qualified loans are not taxable unless you default on repayment. 

When should I begin investing in a TSA?

Now. There is no time like the present. When you start saving may be as important as how much you save. The story to follow explains the power of compounding interest, and the impact of time in a tax deferred annuity.

Julie, Bobby and Mike all are 25 years old and they are considering savings $2,000.00 per year.

Bobby decides to begin saving now. He saves for ten years, then stops. Mike thinks he is too young to worry about retirement and decides to delay savings until he's 35, at which point he'll save until he is 65. Julie decides to start savings now and continue until age 65.

Let's assume that all three savings programs grow at a 6% rate of return and have no taxes taken out.  Amazingly, in our example, Mike would contribute three times as much as Bobby over the years, but he'd retire with only about $7,000 more than Bobby. The Reason? Bobby is starting now. He has compound interest working for him. Julie, of course is the real winner. By starting now and savings until the age 65, Julie would retire with as much as Bobby and Mike combined.

  • JULIE at the age of 65.    $328,095
  • BOBBY at the age of 65   $167,603
  • MIKE at the age of 65      $160,492

The moral of the story, now is the time to take advantage of the power of compounding interest.

How much can I contribute to a TSA (403B)

As of January 1, 2011, you may contribute up to 100% of your salary, to a maximum of $17,500 per year. This limit may be adjusted annually for inflation. Additional contributions in excess of the $17,500 may be allowed if you are age 50 or older, or if you have 15 years of service with your employer. Your agent can help you determine the amount you may contribute.
